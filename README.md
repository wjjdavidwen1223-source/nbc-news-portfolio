## San Jose Students Turn to AI Tools, but Questions About Access and Accuracy Remain

As artificial intelligence tools become more common in classrooms, some students in San Jose say the technology is helping them study faster, organize ideas, and manage heavy workloads. But educators and students also say the growing use of AI raises concerns about accuracy, overreliance, and unequal access.

At schools and universities across the Bay Area, students are using AI-powered tools for tasks such as brainstorming essay ideas, summarizing readings, and checking grammar. For many, the appeal is simple: the tools save time and make large amounts of information easier to process.

“Sometimes students are overwhelmed by the amount of material they have to read and understand,” said one local education advocate. “AI can help break things down, but it cannot replace critical thinking.”

That balance is becoming a major issue as schools try to determine where AI can be useful and where limits are needed. Some educators argue that AI can support learning when it is used responsibly, especially for outlining, revision, and language support. Others worry students may rely on it too heavily and submit work they do not fully understand.

Concerns about accuracy are also growing. AI-generated responses can sound polished and confident while still including incomplete, misleading, or incorrect information. That means students must still verify facts, review sources, and make their own judgments about what is trustworthy.

“There’s a difference between using technology as a tool and letting it do the thinking for you,” said a Bay Area college student. “It can be helpful, but you still have to know what you’re talking about.”

Access is another issue. While some students have regular access to paid tools, personal devices, and stable internet, others do not. That gap could create new inequalities in how students complete assignments and prepare for school or work opportunities.

In San Jose, where education and technology often intersect, the discussion reflects a broader question facing many communities: how to encourage innovation without leaving people behind.

As AI continues to shape how students learn, the conversation around its role in education is likely to grow across the Bay Area.
